## Phase 1: Project Concept and Prototype

**Origin.** Stellar Dominion (originally "Lunar Laser Cross Project") proposes a kilowatt-class emerald-green laser installed on the Moon's north pole, projecting a cross-shaped beam visible to the naked eye across all 48 contiguous United States.

**Spiritual framing.** The project is publicly framed as a cathedral of engineering — not worship, but awe. The epigraph throughout is Ephesians 2:10 (NIV): *"For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do."* This framing is intentional and public-facing. The cross fires on Sundays; first light targets Easter Sunday.

**Prototype scripts.** Two Python scripts were developed early and now live in `prototype/`:

| Script | Purpose | Algorithm |
|---|---|---|
| `lunar-cross-visualization.py` | Scientifically accurate sky diagram at true angular proportions | Matplotlib; Moon at 0.5°, cross at 0.7°, arm thickness 0.05° |
| `doe_pattern.py` | Printable DOE phase mask for tabletop demo | Gerchberg–Saxton iterative Fourier (8 iterations, 2048×2048 output) |

The DOE script enables a sub-$10 tabletop proof: print the grayscale mask on overhead transparency film, shine a laser pointer through it, see the cross on the wall.

## Phase 2: Core Optical and Radiometric Design

**Wavelength choice.** 532 nm (emerald green) sits at the peak of the human photopic response curve — maximum perceived brightness per watt. Frequency-doubled Nd:YAG or direct fiber at 520–530 nm; both commercially available at multi-kW.

**Required irradiance.** To be unmistakably visible against the night sky (≈ magnitude 0):
```
E_min ≈ 1 × 10⁻⁹ W/m²
```

**Cross geometry.** Continental US angular width from Moon:
```
θ_US = arctan(4500 km / 384,400 km) ≈ 0.67°
Cross span: 0.7° (margin)  |  Arm thickness: 0.05°
Total solid angle: Ω ≈ 2.05 × 10⁻⁵ sr
```

**Power calculation.**
```
P_opt = E × Ω × d² = (1×10⁻⁹) × (2.05×10⁻⁵) × (1.477×10¹⁷) ≈ 3.0 kW minimum
Baseline: 5 kW (+4.5 dB margin for atmospheric absorption, DOE losses, pointing error)
Wall-plug at 35% efficiency: ~14.3 kW electrical
```

**DOE.** Fused-silica surface-relief DOE, computed via Gerchberg–Saxton. 80–95% diffraction efficiency; zero-order beam must be blocked. Passive, no power, no moving parts, indefinite vacuum life.

## Phase 3: Architecture — Starship as Permanent Base

**Initial concept (rejected).** Early design used a standalone pallet delivered inside Starship: custom structure, separate power system, pallet lowered to surface. Mass: ~2,306 kg.

**Adopted architecture.** Starship HLS lands permanently on the lunar north pole and *becomes* the operational platform. Key insight: why design a 50 m tower when SpaceX is already delivering one?

| What Starship provides for free | Benefit |
|---|---|
| 50 m stainless steel hull | Structure, rigidity, mounting surface — no custom frame needed |
| Height | d_horizon = √(2 × 1,737,400 × 50) ≈ 13.2 km line-of-sight |
| ~200 t thermal mass | Stabilises laser operating temperature |
| Power bus | Shared infrastructure reduces custom avionics |
| S-band/Ku-band comms | Reduced ground system cost |
| 304L stainless steel in vacuum | No corrosion, no weather, indefinite structural life |

**Deployable boom.** 3–5 m carbon composite boom extends from the nosecone area, carrying the laser/optics/gimbal. From the lunar north pole, Earth is always near the horizon (0° ± 6.7° libration), so the boom points roughly horizontally — a geometrically simple configuration.

**Mass reduction.** Eliminating the pallet (−400 kg) plus switching to SolAero IMM-α panels (−410 kg vs. traditional) reduced total payload from 2,306 kg to **1,357 kg — a 41% reduction**.

## Phase 4: Power System Decisions

### Solar Technology

**Decision record: why not nuclear?**

| Option | Mass (kg) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Solar (SolAero IMM-α, 45 m²) | 90 | Peak of eternal light: 70–95% solar illumination |
| RTG (MMRTG at ~140 W) | ~220 per kW → >2,000 for 17 kW | Impractical at this power level |
| Kilopower fission reactor (10 kW) | ~400 | Unproven on surface, ITAR complications, 10 kW limit per unit |

Solar wins on both mass and cost at polar peaks. RTG is ruled out by power level; reactor by maturity and regulatory complexity.

**Decision record: why not Starlink-heritage silicon cells?**

Investigated after noting SpaceX might have a simpler procurement path via its Starlink supply chain. Finding: not viable.

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| Not commercially available | Starlink solar supply chain is captive and proprietary |
| Wrong technology | Single-junction silicon, ~16–18% AM0 efficiency vs. 33% for IMM-α |
| Radiation degradation | Silicon degrades 2–3%/yr; workable for 5-yr LEO sats, not for 10–20 yr surface install |
| Thermal range | Terrestrial-heritage encapsulants not qualified for lunar day/night swings (−173 to +127°C) |

**Selected: Rocket Lab (SolAero) IMM-α.** Inverted metamorphic multi-junction, 33% AM0 BOL efficiency, 2 kg/m², bare cells on Kapton (no cover glass in vacuum). Commercially available through Rocket Lab Space Systems (acquired SolAero in 2022). Upgrade path: **IMM-β** at 33.3% with improved specific power — drop-in replacement, no array redesign.

**Array sizing.**
```
Required: 17 kW (laser + avionics + thermal + 10% margin)
Output density: 1,361 W/m² × 0.33 = 449 W/m²
Required area: 17,000 / 449 ≈ 37.9 m²
Baseline: 45 m² → 20.2 kW peak (degradation margin)
Mass: 45 × 2 = 90 kg
```

### Battery Technology

**Decision record: why Amprius SiCore?**

| Technology | Cell energy density | Pack estimate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tesla Powerpack (LFP) | ~160 Wh/kg | ~100 Wh/kg | COTS, heavy |
| Standard NMC | ~250 Wh/kg | ~175 Wh/kg | Mature, available |
| Amprius SiCore (silicon-anode) | **400 Wh/kg** | **~280 Wh/kg** | Highest commercially available; aerospace customers |

**Selected: Amprius SiCore.** Battery sized for survival only during eclipses (laser off; no observer can see an eclipsed Moon):
```
E_usable = 1 kW × 72 hr = 72 kWh
At 80% DoD: capacity = 90 kWh
At 280 Wh/kg pack: mass = 90,000 / 280 = 321 kg → baseline 325 kg
```

### Sunday-Only Operation

**Decision record.** The hardware supports continuous operation, but the cross fires only on Sundays. This is a scheduling decision — same hardware as the continuous baseline.

*Could we shrink the array for Sunday-only duty?* Investigated a small solar + large burst-battery scheme (7 m² array charging all week, discharging Sunday). With SolAero at 2 kg/m²:

| Architecture | Solar (kg) | Battery (kg) | Total (kg) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Baseline (45 m² SolAero + survival battery) | 90 | 325 | **415** |
| Small solar (7 m² SolAero) + burst battery | 14 | 675 | **689** |

Baseline is **274 kg lighter** — the opposite of a wash. SolAero's low areal density makes large arrays cheap on mass, removing the incentive for the charging strategy. Baseline retained.

**Benefits of Sunday-only:**

| Benefit | Impact |
|---|---|
| Laser lifetime | ~416 hrs/yr vs. 8,760 → effectively indefinite (>240 yr at rated 100,000 hr) |
| Thermal cycling | Reduced stress on optics and diodes |
| Ground operations | Sunday monitoring saves ~$5–10M over 10-year mission |
| Narrative resonance | Cross appears on the Sabbath; Easter Sunday is the natural inaugural event |

Original tables bundled everything into one total. Restructured to make the "hitch a ride" scenario explicit:

| Category | Low ($M) | High ($M) |
|---|---|---|
| Core project (hardware, integration, test, ground station, PM) | 42 | 114 |
| Starship lunar cargo flight | 20 | 50 |
| **Grand total** | **62** | **164** |
| + 10 years operations | +10 | +28 |
